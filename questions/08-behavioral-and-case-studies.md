# 8. Behavioral & Case Studies

Cross-disciplinary collaboration and real-world research tradeoffs — wetware computing spans neuroscience, electrical engineering, computer science, and bioethics all at once.

### Q: Tell me about a time you had to collaborate with someone from a very different technical background (e.g., a neuroscientist working with a signal-processing engineer, or a wet-lab biologist working with a computer scientist) on a wetware computing project. 🟡

**What a strong answer includes:**
- A specific example showing genuine two-way collaboration — the neurobiological perspective and the engineering/computational perspective each meaningfully shaping the project's direction, rather than one discipline's work happening in isolation.
- Evidence of proactively building shared vocabulary across the neuroscience/engineering divide, given how much specialized terminology exists on both sides.
- A concrete example showing how this collaboration led to a better outcome (e.g., a neuroscientist's insight about culture health meaningfully changing an experimental timeline, or a signal-processing engineer's insight meaningfully improving decoding rigor per section 5).

**Follow-ups:**
- What specific communication practices did you find most effective for bridging this particular collaboration?

### Q: Describe a time an experimental result in a closed-loop stimulation study looked promising initially but didn't hold up under more rigorous scrutiny (e.g., appropriate control conditions or statistical validation). How did you approach investigating and communicating this? 🟡

**What a strong answer includes:**
- Honesty that this is a genuine, common risk in this field given the small-sample-size and statistical-rigor challenges discussed in section 5 — interviewers are wary of candidates who present every promising early result as having held up cleanly.
- A systematic investigation process connecting to the specific rigor concepts discussed in sections 4 and 5 (appropriate control conditions, null-baseline comparison, distinguishing genuine learning from other explanations).
- An honest account of how this was communicated to collaborators or in any resulting publication/report, and what changed about the team's experimental design or statistical practices going forward.

**Follow-ups:**
- How did this experience shape how skeptically you now approach an initially promising-looking result in this field?

### Q: Walk me through how you'd approach a new research project: "investigate whether structured closed-loop stimulation produces activity changes consistent with learning in a newly-established cultured neural network preparation." 🟡

**Case-study structure — a strong candidate would:**
1. **Establish baseline culture validation criteria first** (section 2) — confirming the culture reaches expected functional maturity benchmarks before beginning any experimental manipulation.
2. **Design the closed-loop stimulation protocol with rigorous control conditions built in from the start** (sections 3 and 4) — including a matched non-contingent stimulation control condition, rather than only running the experimental condition and hoping to retrofit adequate controls later.
3. **Plan the statistical analysis approach explicitly upfront**, accounting for the small-sample-size and technical-versus-biological-replicate considerations discussed in section 5, including a clearly defined null-baseline comparison for any decoding or activity-change analysis.
4. **Engage relevant ethical review processes appropriate to the specific research** (section 7), particularly if working with human-derived tissue or organoids of any meaningful complexity, rather than treating this as a late-stage formality.
5. **Plan careful, precisely-scoped result communication and reporting** from the start — deciding in advance what specific claims the planned experimental design will and won't be able to rigorously support, to avoid the temptation toward overstated framing once (if) an interesting result is obtained.

**Follow-ups:**
- Your closed-loop condition shows a statistically significant activity change, but your matched non-contingent control condition shows a smaller but still non-trivial change as well. How would you interpret and report this result?

### Q: Tell me about a time you had to communicate honestly to a stakeholder (a funder, a journalist, or the public) about the genuine limitations or uncertainties in a wetware computing result, given the field's susceptibility to sensationalized coverage discussed throughout this repo. 🟡

**What a strong answer includes:**
- A specific example of honestly conveying genuine scientific progress and its actual, precisely-scoped significance, while being clear about what wasn't demonstrated or remains uncertain (including, where relevant, appropriate caution around moral-status-adjacent claims, per section 7).
- Evidence of proactively anticipating and addressing likely misinterpretations (e.g., preemptively clarifying that an observed activity change doesn't establish sentience or consciousness) rather than only correcting misunderstandings after they arose.
- A concrete, positive outcome showing the audience came away with an accurate, appropriately-calibrated understanding.

**Follow-ups:**
- How do you personally handle a journalist or media outlet's persistent framing of your work in more dramatic or definitive terms than you believe the evidence supports?

### Q: Describe a situation where you had to decide how much precautionary weight to give to moral-status uncertainty (per section 7) in a specific research design decision, given genuine scientific uncertainty and no clear institutional precedent to follow. 🔴

**What a strong answer includes:**
- A clear, specific articulation of the actual tradeoff considered — connecting to the precautionary-principle discussion in section 7 — without asserting that either a strongly precautionary or a more measured approach is simply "correct."
- Evidence of engaging relevant institutional ethical review and colleagues' perspectives genuinely, rather than making a unilateral judgment call in a genuinely contested area.
- An honest, reflective account of the reasoning process and outcome, including acknowledgment of the genuine difficulty and uncertainty involved, rather than presenting the decision as obviously correct in hindsight.

**Follow-ups:**
- How do you think about your own personal responsibility in a field where institutional and field-wide ethical norms are still actively being developed, rather than settled and simply requiring compliance?
